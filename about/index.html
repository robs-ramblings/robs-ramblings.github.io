About

This is me, and for some reason in most photos, I have a beer in my hand!


I grew up in the north of England, was child of the 1970’s, and part of the awesome home computer scene that thrived in the UK in the 1980’s. I always liked the Commodore machines, and later I would get an Amiga, but my first computer was the legendary BBC Micro. I really have to thank my parents because they must have sold a kidney as the BBC was really expensive, compared to say a ZX Spectrum, without the BBC I might have not learned to program.

1980’s

Learning to program back then was simple, fun and the feedback was immediate, something that sorely missing today. I started with BBC BASIC and then 6502 machine code. I didn’t have a floppy disk drive for many years, it was all cassette tapes and our house didn’t have the most reliable power! Lots of code was lost and lots was redone. I learned from the school of hard knocks that the second time you do something its usually better.

This was the 1980’s and there was no internet, you had to figure things out for yourself. Everything was learned through friends, peers and local computer clubs. I wish we still had the same thing; you would find books, magazines and people who knew more than you. Everybody had their own plans as to what they were going to do with these new home computers, exploration was encouraged, there were no wrong answers. I was fascinated with graphics and audio which generally pointed to making games and later on the demo scene. It wasn’t long before I was abusing the hardware, racing the beam down the screen and writing software at the bare metal - something that has stuck with me to this day.

My first published piece of code was in one of the Acorn magazines, can’t remember which, it was a 3D extension to the BBC Basic Plot command. It didn’t do much, just the math to convert from 3D to 2D and then used the existing plot features, it was pretty cool as it allowed you to draw lines and triangles in 3D. I think going rate for this type of article was about £50, as a teenager it was awesome.

Around 1987, while still in school, I got access to an Acorn Archimedes A310, with the new and revolutionary ARM processor. I jumped right in to learn ARM assembly, it was so logical and easy to learn but it was hard to master.

1990’s

A few years later I managed to buy myself an Acorn A3000, by this time I was pretty fluent in ARM assembly. The 8Mhz ARM2 was plenty fast enough to software render 2D and 3D graphics at TV resolutions. I got through college writing bits of game tech for others, interrupt driven audio mixers, tracker players, collision systems, 2D sprite renderers and 3D triangle renderers - all in fixed point math because floating point didn’t exist.

Around this time I also bought myself an Amiga which was probably the best programmers machine ever made, to this day I have fond memories of programming the Amiga, it was so different than the 100% software approach of the Acorn. There was lots of debate as to which machine was better, was it the hardware in the Amiga or the fast processor in the Archimedes? I still don’t know the answer. Most game programmers from this period would take other games apart just to see what they did and how they worked. This made game programmers pretty good at cracking, I was no different, I got pretty good at cracking games on both the Archimedes and the Amiga. Being a teenager I didn’t think about the implications, it was all punk rock and anarchy, but it was educational. Reverse engineering the disk protection of a competitors game, just to see what they did so you could do it better, was better than any college course.

I made it all the way through college without ever owning a PC, I could do everything I needed on the Acorn or Amiga. The Acorn was paying the college bills (aka bar tab)so I didn’t need a PC and I probably couldn’t afford one. I was doing lots of game related stuff on the Acorn but it didn’t have joystick support in the same way the Amiga did. The OS (RiscOS) supported the idea of a joystick but there was no way to connect one. One day I had this idea to hook up a SNES game controller to the parallel port. It used a simple serial bit stream and a clock and if you put the other data lines high there would be enough current to power the controller! I wrote an RiscOS module that would read the gamepad directly from the printer port and then emulate the existing joystick interface, as well as virtually press keys - which allowed game that didn’t have any joystick support to work with the controller. It was called the PowerPad and sold by a company called Eclipse. This was my first hardware product and a few other things for the Acorn followed, the most popular was an audio sampler that also plugged in to the printer port.

After college I ended up at Krisalis software in Rotherham, England. Most of the really good Archimedes games came out of Krisalis and a lot of those games were Amiga ports and written in assembler, I fit right in. It was during my time at Krisalis that I first got a PC, it was a shiny new 90Mhz Pentium. The OG Pentium was a fun machine to program and about the last that made sense to program in assembler.

In 1996, after I had been at Krisalis for a number of years, I moved to Los Angeles. Moving from a northern England town to Hollywood was quite a culture shock. I worked on Trespasser at Dreamworks which was another big change for me. It was C++ and Windows and it was really the first time I had worked on a big team developing a completely new game. Even more crazy was to randomly bump in to Steven Spielberg around the office! Trespasser never lived up to the hype, the game play was legendary for all the wrong reasons, but the tech we developed was ground breaking. We had bump maps and per pixel diffuse and specular lighting, we had tools that would encode surface curvature from high resolution meshes in to the bump maps of low resolution meshes - things that would not be main stream for many more years. The software rendering had perspective correction, sub pixel correction, bilinear filtering, per pixel fog, a semi volumetric sky. We had real time audio foley so as different physics materials collided it would make physically correct sounds, I actually won a game developer award in 1997 or 1998 for the real time foley audio tech! It had rigid body physics and water, we encoded the ripples in the water as bump maps so it looked awesome. The terrain utilized wavelets to produce dynamic resolution. To render the forests we had an image cache system that would render groups of trees as imposters to 2D textures and then simply warp the texture back to the scene - all stuff that is fairly high end today and this was 30 years ago and without a GPU!

When I first got there the game was in quite a sorry state as far as performance and memory consumption went, my job was to optimize it and optimize it I did! The original renderer was a templated C++ monster - this exact piece of code is where my hatred for C++ comes from, one day I’ll write an article about it. I rewrote the entire thing in assembler and optimized it for Pentium, Pentium MMX, Pentium Pro and at some point AMD became a launch partner and I had to last minute optimize it for the AMD K6 and the ‘3D Now!’ instruction set.

Unfortunately the game was a victim of circumstance, along with some bad management. After being delayed multiple times, it put the game in a time frame of PC graphics rapidly adopting hardware. At this moment in time the 3dfx was king - we had no choice but to support hardware rendering. It was an unholy mix. The hardware at the time was much faster than our software renderer and could support higher resolutions but couldn’t do anything similar to the features we were doing in software. Trespasser was probably the last software rendered PC game and it would have been a technological masterpiece if it had been released 2-3 years earlier.

The sad part is it could have been a great game if it had been a Turok style dino shooter from day one, instead of the cluster it became. It has a cult following today, its been reverse engineered and people make new content for it! It was a cool game to work on and the technology was impressive enough to get the attention of Microsoft, who at the time was making Talisman, a hardware tile rendering system that predates Direct3D, very similar to our imposter/image caching system, but our system was much better. It was faster and better, even in software.

2000’s

Dreamworks is where I first met Seamus Blackley and he and I both went to Microsoft where we worked on the original XBox. The Xbox is a well known story, there isn’t much that hasn’t been said. I worked on the general system architecture but mostly the graphics and audio. I was involved in the revolt against Gigapixel and got a nVidia GPU in there and probably saved Xbox from the same fate as other Microsoft consumer products - remember WebTV??. We were called crazy at the time for making a console out of PC parts, its kinda funny how things work out as now all the consoles are PC hardware!.

While at Microsoft I found myself working with Mike Abrash who’s VGA book had been a bible to me many years earlier while programming the Pentium. Another thing I did at Xbox was to form the ATG group, to this day the ATG group still exists and still does awesome work.

After Xbox I started a consulting company, it was my first time working for my own company. My plan was to help developers with XBox games, at this point in time I knew a lot about the Xbox that developers didn’t. One thing I didn’t anticipate was being exposed to the Playstation 2 which I had been forced to avoid. I liked the PS2 and programming it reminded me of the Amiga, it was a real programmers machine and it was esoteric, in the right hands was more powerful than Xbox. The esoteric nature of the PS2 along with lots of assembler made porting code to Xbox quite difficult, I was there to help.

The initial consulting company didn’t last too long, in the process of helping developers with their PS2 to Xbox conversions, I met Ted Price and ended up working at Insomniac Games, and I don’t really remember how it happened because Insomniac had nothing to do with the Xbox. At this point Insomniac was about 20 people and needed an engine programmer. Funnily enough my apartment and Insomniac were about 1/4 mile apart, we were both on Barham Blvd in North Hollywood.

Insomniac was, and still is, an absolutely amazing place to work - it’s one of the few places I’ve worked where I would go back in a heartbeat. That says a lot because its been 25+ years since I first started there. Working on the PS2 engine for the Ratchet and Clank games was one of the things I enjoyed most in my career. This era was the pinnacle of traditional console development.

Sony at this point were designing the PS3 and Insomniac was invited very early on to be part of it. Mark Cerny formed a group called the Sony ICE team, it was kinda like the ATG group at Microsoft. The ICE team was located at the Naughty Dogs offices in Santa Monica, I was there full time. We had great access to the PS3, we’d go to Japan and get special access to development hardware and we’d to get use the cycle level simulators and emulators. This was long before the PS3 resembled anything similar to what was finally released. At this moment in time the PS3 still had a Sony GPU, the nVidia RSX wasn’t even a pipedream - there is a whole story here about how nVidia got in the PS3 that needs to be told.

I was technically on loan to the ICE team, I was still employed by Insomniac and I went back to them fulltime when they started developing plans for their first PS3 game, this game would ultimately be Resistance Fall of Man, and it needed a new engine. The PS2 engine was very specific to the PS2, it had quite terrible tooling and while the game code was C, the engine was more or less straight MIPS assembler - there was no way any of it could be used. For the new engine it was decided that it would be more extendable and it all be C/C++ and we’d optimize from there, as needed. I was architect of this new PS3 engine, at this point I probably knew more about the PS3 than anybody alive. I had a secret office at Insomniac that was locked to the outside world and even other employees, in this office was one of the first development kits in the United States.

I worked on all of the Insomniac PS3 games, modifying the engine for the various Ratchet and Resistance games. There was a surprising amount of work to support two very different games with a dedicated engine, especially moving from the launch version of Resistance running at 30hz to Ratchet and Clank at 60hz. In about 2008 I moved to Boulder Colorado and restarted my consulting company, I did a lot of remote work for Insomniac and this lasted for many years. I worked on what became their PS4 and that became the current PS5 engine.

2010’s

Even after Insomniac, Ratchet and Clank still played a big roll in my work as I did the Ratchet and Clank HD collection - remaking the original PS2 games on the PS3. It was fun project, probably only possible because I wrote the original, some random third party developer would have had nightmares. I got to work on lots of AAA games, the Xbox hardware and the PS3 hardware but it was time to take a break from games.

While consulting I worked for lots of really cool companies, big and small. I worked for a drone startup doing aerial odometry and navigation for startup that became Zipline. Another that stands out is OTOY who started the none gaming chapter of my career.

At this time OTOY were doing lots of early work in path tracing on GPUs, distributing working across multiple GPUs in the cloud, recording 360 video for VR headsets, even designing custom camera hardware and rigs to mount them on. If you want to interact with this rendered data you need to stream more than just color buffers. I was doing research on codecs for streaming compressed depth data, to hide the round trip latency to the cloud a predicting/warping system - this was years before such a thing was used in VR and AR. OTOY did a lot of work with movies and operated the original LightStage - I was manipulating and extracting lighting data from series of still images.

Movie graphics and special effects are more of a process, a whole world away from game graphics and certainly not realtime. I spent a lot of time trying to make movie graphics faster so we could at least offer realtime previews without waiting for final frames to be rendered. It was all about realism as fast as possible, where games are all about speed as real as possible - one day soon these worlds will meet in the middle.

I was never really interested in VR but AR is fascinating. Integrating realistic 3D graphics in to the real world is what movies do but for AR you have to do it in real time. AR basically combined everything I had done in my career into one place, there was rendering, lighting, cameras, sensors. I went to Magic Leap in the very early days. I worked on the platform architecture, software architecture, custom display hardware and the core graphics - pretty much everything other than the optics in the MagicLeap 1. I kicked out Qualcomm and put the Nvidia X1 in there - the third time I had been involved in getting nVidia in to a device. It was a challenge to see what was possible, with the inherent overhead of AR the graphics quality remained looking like 1990’s graphics! The hardest part wasn’t technical, it was managing expectations of management, who would release videos of things that were entirely impossible.

I briefly worked on AR graphics hardware and holographic displays at Daqri where I got to work with Seamus again.

2020’s

It didn’t last long as Daqri went out of business. I guess I was in the right place and the right time as Apple came knocking at my door.

I stayed in the world of AR working on the platform architecture of VisonPro in the TDG group, having fingers in lots of pies from custom silicon and deeply embedded firmware to GPU rendering and user interactions. If anybody could make AR work it would be Apple! Right? The vertical integration aspect of Apple is amazing, the fact that you can get silicon made or changed to fix something like a user interface problem is unique to them. The Vison Pro had 14 cameras and each had its own image processor, I spent a lot of time deal with data transfers, synchronization and presenting camera frames to applications. We came up with a whole new programming model where code only runs when all the input buffers are ready.

It’ll be interesting to see where Apple goes with AR, can come back from the high cost of the Vision Pro and make a real dent in the consumer AR space after so many have tried and failed? At this point a lot of the technical AR problems, things like sticking pixels to the world are solved, frame rates are high latency is low. Now it becomes a problem of understanding the incoming light, then environment you are in and what you’re looking at - all things that local AI can help with. The movie quality graphics will have to wait until this is solved.

Apple is a hard place to work, the way they do things is foreign to anybody who has worked anywhere else, product security is everywhere and gets in its own way, lots of work gets replicated because you can’t talk to the person who wrote the original code because they don’t know your project exists. After spending a good few years there, I wouldn’t work there again, I was hesitant to go in the first place.

Most recently I worked on a custom avionics stack for Nighthawk flight systems. This is a fully certified, Linux based, primary flight display with realistic terrain rendering at very high frame rates. I was the software architect for the entire Linux stack, on top of that a custom graphics architecture and the thing had to be certified by the FAA.

This is my story of 40 years of hands on development, I’ve seen a lot of change, but have never stopped programming or staying on the cutting edge - some times it works out and some times it doesn’t. To this day you’ll find me on the front lines of development and optimization and the frontline is where I belong. I guess learning ARM assembly 40 years ago did pay off.

Today I live in Boulder Colorado and when I’m not doing tech related stuff you’ll find me teaching skydiving or flying planes at Longmont airport. I am hopeful that the next generation of developers are still interested in throwing away another generic framework, and peaking behind the curtain to see how things work.
